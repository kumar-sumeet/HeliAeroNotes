The lift distributions over the rotor disk is central to how the good a rotor is - this is true both in hover as well as in forward flight. Again, good here can refer to acoustic noise generated by the rotor, total power consumption, or vibratory loads caused by the rotor. Any sort of aerodynamic loading on the rotor blades is a source of noise and depending on the nature of the distribution the noise can be higher or lower. Aerodynamic loading (or airloads) is not the only contributor to rotor noise but since this is not the focus of this course Iâll resist the temptation to talk further about it. Similarly, high frequency structural loads transferred from the rotor to the fuselage can be felt by the pilot/passengers as uncomfortable vibrations and can be reduced if the rotor is designed with a specific lift distribution. Gone are the days of yore when limited manufacturing capabilities meant that only constant-chord rectangular blades with symmetric airfoils could be constructed. Not only can blade planforms be molded into fancy shapes now but also shape adaptive structure can be installed on them to allow âactiveâ optimisation of rotor performance.

Modern rotors in this chapter does not necessarily refer to chronologically the most recent designs. Instead, it refers to rotor designs that include state-of-the-art and beyond state-of-the-art technologies. Some of these technologies were actually tested (and even flight-tested in some cases) nearly 4 decades ago. The principle aim of these technolgies was to push the operational envelope of helicopters or improve rotor efficiency within the operational envelope. Efficiency here refers to the three major aspects of rotor operation - power consumption, vibration, and acoustics. Clearly, there are aspects of the blade design that influence rotor performance - for e.g. taper, twist, airfoil, tip sweep, chord/solidity distribution etc. Weâve already seen how finding the optimum set of such design parameters that lead to best rotor performance is a matter of compromise between hover and forward flight performance. In general, this is considered to be an exercise in passive rotor optimisation since the rotor performance is established apriori and cannot be influenced during operation. Passive optimisation occurs during the rotor design phase and the final rotor design is then fixed and usually ends up being a compromise between conflicting requirements, the most optimum design overall nonetheless. However, if there was a way to change rotor design parameters at different operating states then that would clearly provide an added degree of freedom to influence rotor efficiency and potentially even improve it further. But how would one go on to carry out such an excercise - replacing the blades of a rotor, from highly twisted to blades with low twist, mid-flight!? Installing different rotors on the helicopter based on whether the mission on a given day involved more hover or forward flight operation is not really practical either! A more fundamental question to ask at this point would be how exactly does a particular blade geometric feature help in specific flight condition and whether that can be achieved via an alternate mechanism.

This chapter refers to rotor designs and/or constitutive technologies that help improve rotorcraft within the scope of these parameters. In particular, the focus is on the following-

  • advancing blade concept (ABC) design

  • higher harmonic blade control

    • higher harmonic control (HHC) mechanism

    • individual blade control (IBC) mechanism

    • active trailing-edge flap (TEF) control

  • compound helicopter designs

The constitutive components of power consumption are, by now, apparent for all operational flight regimes. The main rotor is the biggest source of vibration experienced within the aircraft fuselage. In case of a perfectly balanced rotor, these vibrations are felt at frequencies that are dictated by the number of blades and the rate of rotation of the rotor. However, manufacturing tolerances dictate that not all blade (even on the same rotor) are exactly alike - both aerodynamically and structurally. This can potentially further increase vibratory loads within the fuselage. Aeroacoustic noise of a body is mainly dictated by its speed relative to the air (Mach number) and the pressure distribution over its surface. Given that rotor blade tips can reach Mach numbers of up to 0.9 in high-speed forward flight, they can be a signficant source of noise. Additionally, high noise can be caused during low speed flight as well - for e.g. when BVI occurs during descent flight. This is due to the sudden change in lift distribution (which is in turn a result of change in pressure distribution) over the blade that occurs when in hits the tip vortex from a preceding blade.

The ability to control the blade pitch only at 1/rev via the swashplate is a severe restriction when it comes to phenomena that occur on the rotor disc at frequencies >1/rev.
